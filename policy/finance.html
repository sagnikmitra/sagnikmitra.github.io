Executive summary

Key message: the 2026-27 Budget sets the right direction; this dossier supplies the delivery mechanisms that secure each commitment — most by re-instrumenting money already budgeted.

¶1. The Budget commits to entrepreneurship, a ₹5,000 crore investment-promotion framework, a single-window system, a State Land Bank, legislation against unlawful business interference, a 24×7 urban economy, and cooperative federalism [1]. Twelve proposals follow — ten strengthening announced commitments, two addressing adjacent revenue opportunities.

¶2. Five audited findings frame them:

1. The fifty-year context. West Bengal's per-capita income rank fell from 3rd among states (1960-61) to 24th (2023-24) [2]; its share of India's industrial output from 9.8% (1980-81) to 5.0% (1997-98) [3]; and the official Annual Survey of Industries places WB outside the top five states on every principal characteristic — factories, employment, GVA, fixed capital — throughout 2010-11→2023-24, with its GVA share eroding from ~3.0% to ~2.5% [4]. Cumulative FDI stands near US$1.95 billion against Maharashtra's ~US$94 billion [5]. WB exports ~2.9% of India's merchandise on ~5.8% of India's GDP — an export-to-GDP-share ratio of ~0.5 [6][D1]. 2. The receipts architecture, decomposed. The Budget requires revenue receipts to rise +30.9% in one year [1][D2]. The official budget-document family shows the composition: own state tax +16.94%; grants-in-aid +223.5% (₹22,069 → ₹71,393 crore) [7]. Against this, the RBI's own-tax series for WB shows a nine-year CAGR of 9.98% and a buoyancy of 1.03 [8][D3] — so the plan rides on central-transfer realisation, which the Budget's cooperative stance is designed to secure and Proposal R1 is designed to protect. 3. Fiscal space is thin on the audited record. Committed expenditure (salaries+pensions+interest) has run at ~65% of revenue receipts on actuals (all-states: 53%) [9][10]; total debt service consumes 30.5% of budgeted receipts [1][D4]; roughly half of fresh borrowing services old principal (₹54,606.53 cr repayment against ₹1,05,388 cr gross) [1][D5]. The CAG has reported ₹2.29 lakh crore of grants without utilisation certificates and majority use of market borrowings to balance the revenue account in an audited year [11]. 4. Under-spending, not under-allocation, is the historical bottleneck. CAG Appropriation-Account analysis shows 57.69% of a ₹3,002 crore MGNREGA central-share provision, 64.84% of a Krishak Bandhu provision, and 62% of a TPDS rice provision unspent in audited years [12]. Every proposal names its delivery apparatus, not merely its outlay. 5. Credit starvation of the enterprise base is measured. WB holds ~89 lakh MSMEs — 21.5% of India's units, the largest state count [13] — yet bank credit works out to ≈₹1.63 lakh per unit per year [13][D6]; priority-sector Annual Credit Plan achievement is 60% [14]; and five districts sit 22-25 percentage points below the state credit-deposit ratio [14][D7].

¶3. The five highest-leverage moves: (R2) convert Banglar Udyam's ₹200 crore into a first-loss guarantee corpus → ₹2,000-3,000 crore of lending capacity, piloted in the five lowest-CD districts; (R5) a 2.5%-of-GSDP capital-outlay floor financed through the Budget's own ₹10,000 crore SASCI target; (R3+R7) a statutory deemed-approval clock, a public MoU-grounding dashboard, and a published semiconductor incentive schedule; (R1) a statutory mid-year revenue-realisation report; (R11) municipal property-tax modernisation — Kolkata's ₹1,258.53 crore collection against Bengaluru's ₹4,930 crore [15] is the largest untapped urban revenue pool.

¶5. The ASI arithmetic deserves emphasis because it is the government of India's own industrial census: between 2010-11 and 2023-24, all-India factory GVA grew at ~8.4%/yr while WB's share of it declined — WB's factories and workforce grew with the national tide, but value-added grew slower: a shrinking slice of a growing pie [4][D8]. The export series tells the same story from trade: WB's top export categories — jewellery (US$1,452.9mn), iron & steel (US$1,291.9mn), leather goods (US$648.3mn) [18] — sit well below the state's economic weight [D1].

¶6. The historical record rests on primary documents: the Rajya Sabha's 1989 freight-equalisation answer, in which the framing of "discrimination against the Eastern States" was effectively conceded as the policy was phased out [19]; the EPW's 1967 study of the gherao movement [20]; the Supreme Court's Singur holding (31 Aug 2016) that the acquisition for a private company did not constitute "public purpose" (Kedar Nath Yadav v. State of West Bengal) [21]; and the arc from Wallace's 1909 observation that jute was roughly half of Bengal's exports at peak [22] to the Rajya Sabha's 1993 jute-slump answer [23]. Economists writing today converge on a narrower diagnosis: the decline is concentrated in industry, with governance and labour-relations factors as persistent causes [24][25].

I.2 What the ground reports — and what the Budget already answers

¶7. Qualitative sentiment (anonymous public forums; directional colour, not representative data): the recurring public narrative is that unlawful demands on lawful business — historically through militant industrial action, more recently through informal levies — have made exit the rational choice for firms and founders [26]. The Budget itself addresses this directly: it commits to "legislation to safeguard lawful businesses from 'syndicate charges' and other informal extortions" [1, pp.60-61]. Comparable statutes elsewhere show the lawful drafting pattern: conduct declared unlawful in the abstract, offence provisions cast on "whoever contravenes," and due-process safeguards throughout — the approach of the Gujarat Land Grabbing (Prohibition) Act 2020 and the Maharashtra Protection of Interest of Depositors Act 1999 [27][28]. Proposals R3, R4 and R7 supply the administrative machinery that converts this legislative commitment into investor confidence.

¶9. Four structural reads, computations in Annex 1: 1. Composition of the receipts plan: own-tax +16.94%, grants-in-aid +223.5% [7]; the FRBM statement's tax chapter attributes only ~₹1,000 crore to new measures [7]. WB's own-tax nine-year CAGR is 9.98% (buoyancy 1.03) on RBI series [8][D3], and the FY26 SGST season illustrates variance risk: the revised estimate itself missed by ₹1,464 crore before an +8.5% target was set [45]. Conclusion: the +30.9% is a transfers-realisation plan; protect it with measurement (R1) and the conditionality checklist (Part IV). 2. Expenditure expands simultaneously: Annapurna Yojana ₹36,000 crore — arithmetically exactly 1.00 crore beneficiaries at ₹36,000/year [1][D9]; DA +20 points to 38% from 1 Oct 2026 [1]; VB-G RAM G ₹14,000 crore; PMAY-G ₹13,000 crore; one lakh vacancy fills [1]. 3. Debt service: ₹97,640.50 crore = 30.5% of budgeted receipts [D4]; rollover 51.8% [D5]; guarantees outstanding ₹12,840-17,705 crore/yr FY22-26, within the statutory ceiling [7]. 4. True capital outlay ≈1.9% of GSDP vs the 2.2-2.8% all-states band [46][D10]; the NIPFP multiplier (capex 2.45 vs ~1.0 for revenue spending) makes the R5 floor the growth strategy [47].

PART II — TWELVE PROPOSALS

Format per the group directive: what to do · why · precedent · Viksit Bengal impact · cost. Each opens with the Budget commitment it builds on. Recommendations numbered R1-R12.

R1 — A Revenue Realism Framework and GST-analytics enforcement cell

Builds on: the Budget's consolidation path and next-stage Public Financial Management reforms [1, p.6]. The gap. Receipts must grow +30.9% (composition: [7]) after an 8.0% RE shortfall [1][D11]; own-tax history runs ~10%/yr [8]; ~65% of receipts are pre-committed on actuals [9][10]. No public mid-year tracking exists. Proposal. (a) A statutory mid-year revenue-realisation report (tabled by 30 November) tracking every receipt head, including central-transfer realisation. (b) A GST & E-Way-Bill Analytics Cell cross-referencing e-way bills with FASTag movement and PAN-linked filings. Why. Closing WB's own-tax/GSDP gap to the all-states average (5.5%→6.8% [46]) is worth ≈₹28,000 crore of recurring capacity [D12] without touching a rate; early visibility protects capital outlay from bearing any correction. Precedent. Maharashtra's analytics-led enforcement lifted GST collections 14.6% to ₹1.87 lakh crore in FY26, a fourth straight year above target [48]; Telangana's toll-data cross-referencing exposed a ₹100 crore fake-invoice case [49]. Viksit Bengal impact. Revenue credibility funds every Shakti; a published report operationalises the Budget's transparency pledge. Cost/impact. Setup ₹40-80 crore; +0.3pp own-tax/GSDP ≈ ₹6,400 crore/yr [D12].

R2 — Banglar Udyam as a credit-guarantee corpus; a district CD-ratio campaign

Builds on: the Banglar Udyam Credit Card (₹200 crore) — the first youth-enterprise credit instrument in a WB budget [1, p.55] — and MSME & Textiles at ₹1,814.60 crore [1]. The gap. The announced per-head scale (2 lakh youth × up to ₹10 lakh) exceeds direct-benefit arithmetic (₹200 cr ÷ 2 lakh = ₹10,000/head) [D13]; the enterprise base is credit-thin: ₹1.63 lakh/unit/yr [D6], ACP priority-sector 60% [14], five districts 22-25 points below the state CD ratio [14][D7]. Proposal. (a) Constitute the ₹200 crore as a first-loss guarantee corpus co-guaranteed with CGTMSE (leverage table in Annex 1: 10% first-loss → ₹2,000 crore of lending). (b) District CD-ratio targets through the SLBC, with pilots in S 24 Parganas, Jhargram, Birbhum, Hooghly, N 24 Parganas [14]. (c) Common Facility Centres in the distress clusters under MSE-CDP's 70-90% grant window [50]. Why. A guarantee multiplies public money 10-15×; a grant divides it. Credit flows where premises and contracts are secure — the compound move with the Budget's own safeguard legislation [1]. Precedent. CGTMSE: ₹5 lakh crore cumulative guarantees across 76 lakh MSEs [51]; MSE-CDP CFC window [50]. Viksit Bengal impact. The 89-lakh-enterprise base — the Budget's "backbone of the state's economy" [1] — becomes fundable at scale. Cost/impact. Cost-neutral (re-instruments the ₹200 crore); ₹2,000-3,000 crore of lending capacity.

R3 — Statutory deemed approval for the Single Window

Builds on: the single-window pledge and the ₹100 crore+ exemption from separate local-body NOCs, processed "in a time-bound manner" [1, p.58]; the Expert Committee reporting within four months [1]. Proposal. Legislate deemed approval — 15 working days (mega projects) / 30 days (others), automatic approval on lapse, an administrative-accountability provision for delay — with the portal as the single legal system of record. Why. A statutory clock converts announcements into ground-breakings. Tamil Nadu's electronics exports rose from US$1.86bn (FY22) to US$14.65bn (FY25) — 41% of India's — on a single-window agency with dedicated investor desks [52]; Vietnam's provincial one-stop licensing underpins US$38.2bn/yr of registered FDI, with Samsung alone at 13.4% of national exports [53][54]. Precedent. Telangana's TS-iPASS (Act of 2014): deemed approval with time-bound administrative accountability [55]; Andhra Pradesh's Single Desk: 39+ clearances in 21 days [56]. Viksit Bengal impact. Contractual certainty — the credibility instrument a post-Singur investment climate needs. Cost/impact. Portal ₹50-75 crore within the ₹5,000 crore framework [1].

R4 — A digital, auditable State Land Bank and the ULCRA white paper

Builds on: the Budget's land-bank commitment with resumption mechanics specified ("refund of the lease premium… without any interest") [1, p.59] and its re-examination of the Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulation) Act 1976, which the Budget identifies as "a major investment bottleneck" [1, p.56]. Proposal. A GIS-tagged, encumbrance-linked land bank with a public, CAG-auditable allotment ledger; a codified resumption SOP (notice → hearing → refund → re-pooling); the ULCRA review as a published white paper with transitional provisions on the pattern used by repealing states. Why. Idle public land is the cheapest industrial input the state owns; transparent process makes resumption legally durable. The Singur holding [21] is the standing reminder that land process is investment policy. Precedent. Telangana's TSIIC land bank: ~53,000 acres developed, web-based allotment, ~1,964 acres resumed from ~225 allottees — with the CAG's transparency critique as the design lesson [57][58]; Gujarat GIDC's touchless online allotment [59]. Viksit Bengal impact. Rule-based land governance, auditable end-to-end. Cost/impact. ₹80-120 crore digitisation; lease premiums and rent on thousands of acres.

R5 — A 2.5%-of-GSDP capital-outlay floor financed by the Budget's own SASCI target

Builds on: the 54.8% jump in true capital outlay, the explicit ₹10,000 crore SASCI target with named reform areas [1, p.18], and the capital pipeline (Dandanpatrabarh deep-sea port, Kalyani airport, Santhaldih 2×800 MW, ₹2,000 crore Bakreshwar floating solar) [1]. The gap. Outlay ≈1.9% of GSDP vs the 2.2-2.8% band [46][D10]; in an audited year 58.84% of market borrowings financed the revenue account [11]. Proposal. A binding capital-outlay floor of 2.5% of GSDP in the FRBM statement; maximum SASCI drawdown against the named checklist; a rolling three-year plan. The port arithmetic strengthens the pipeline case: Mundra handled 200.7 MMT in FY25 — 3.0× SMP Kolkata's best-ever 66.4 MMT — while Haldia's 8.5 m draft caps loads and costs ~₹400 crore/yr in dredging [60][61][62]. Why. NIPFP's estimates: capital-expenditure multiplier 2.45 versus 0.98-0.99 for revenue spending [47]. Precedent. SASCI already finances >25% of capital outlay in 14 states [46]. Viksit Bengal impact. The durable exit from the high-committed-expenditure equilibrium. Cost/impact. +0.6pp of GSDP ≈ ₹12,900 crore → ≈₹31,600 crore of output at the multiplier [D14]; SASCI-financed, near-zero interest.

R6 — A GI Monetisation Cell for the 59 tags, wired to the sector register

Builds on: PM Ekta Mall (₹150 crore), Tex-RAMPS, the Common Tea Processing Centre (₹100 crore), the tea-tourism land-cap correction (30%→15%), and the Durga Puja global-branding campaign [1]. The gap. 59 GI tags — the third-highest state count — with no unified enforcement or brand machinery [63]; an estimated 75-85% of "Darjeeling" sold worldwide is not genuine [64]; protected handloom articles are openly produced on powerlooms [65]. Proposal. A State GI Cell: testing infrastructure on the import corridor; producer-FPO and export linkage; a unified "Bengal GI" mark launched through the Durga Puja campaign; handloom-reservation enforcement task forces. Why. Enforced GIs transform producer prices — authentic Darjeeling commands US$15-40/kg vs US$2.50-3.50 generic (5-12×) [64]. Precedent. France's INAO appellation system: €42bn/yr of protected-origin sales, ~400,000 jobs [66]; Basmati under APEDA+GI: US$5.94bn exports 2024-25 [67]. Viksit Bengal impact. Civilizational heritage converted to rural export income. Cost/impact. ₹100-150 crore (partly within Ekta Mall); premium capture across 59 lines.

R7 — A public MoU-grounding dashboard and a published semiconductor incentive schedule

Builds on: the ₹5,000 crore incentive framework, the Durgapur semiconductor ambition, the GCC policy, and the live pipeline (Berger ₹600 crore, Peerless ₹1,100 crore, L&T data centre with 25,000 jobs, PwC) [1][68]. The gap. No public conversion tracking; and semiconductor competition is explicit — Micron's Sanand terms were ISM 50% + Gujarat ~20%, the investor funding ~30% of US$2.75bn [69]; Tata's Assam OSAT is ₹27,000 crore with ~15,000 direct jobs [70]. Proposal. (a) A live dashboard: signed → land → construction → operational, named conversion officer, quarterly public ratio. (b) A published incentive schedule for semiconductor/data-centre projects matching the Gujarat/Assam stack. Why. Tamil Nadu publishes 72% grounding (722 of 897 MoUs, ₹10.27 lakh crore) and uses it as marketing [71]; the CAG-audited counter-case (renewables MoUs converting at 7.35%) shows the cost of silence [72]. Viksit Bengal impact. A state that grounds what it signs — measured and compounding. Cost/impact. ₹10-20 crore; the return is the conversion delta on the pipeline.

R8 — Full e-registration and e-stamping

Builds on: ₹50 crore for registration reforms: online verification, slot-booking, biometric facial recognition, paperless refunds [1, p.6]. Proposal. Scale to end-to-end e-registration/e-stamping: online duty payment, digitised valuation, developer-office registration, 85%+ online target. Precedent. Maharashtra IGR: ₹50,142.77 crore (FY24) with ~85% online processing [73]; Karnataka's Kaveri 2.0: ₹20,287 crore and ~10-minute turnaround [74]. Viksit Bengal impact. Own-revenue strength without a rate change. Cost/impact. Within the ₹50 crore; double-digit uplift typical on digitisation [73][74].

R9 — Excise track-and-trace alongside the new distance rules

Builds on: the Budget's protective distance rules (1 km from educational/medical/religious institutions; 500 m in KMC) [1, pp.69-70]. Proposal. An integrated supply-chain system — GPS movement tracking, digital locks, QR-coded bottles, PoS retail, e-lottery allotment — so revenue rises from leakage recovery while outlets are restricted. Precedent. Uttar Pradesh: record ₹57,722.26 crore (FY26, +9.79%) on a digitised chain [75]; Karnataka's duty-led episode (volumes −13.74%, target missed) argues for the leakage route [76]. Viksit Bengal impact. Social objective and exchequer served together. Cost/impact. ₹100-150 crore; 5-8% leakage recovery.

R10 — Fund the Profession-Tax relief by widening the base

Builds on: substantial PT relief — thresholds doubled, decriminalisation, BSF exemption [1, pp.67-68]. Proposal. (a) Auto-enrolment of professionals from GST/e-Shram/professional-body rolls as thresholds rise; (b) Motor-Vehicle-Tax modernisation on the Vahan platform (lifetime + green tax). Precedent. Karnataka vehicle tax: ₹10,611 crore → ₹13,000 crore in two years on Vahan-based collection [74]. Viksit Bengal impact. Relief and revenue reconciled. Cost/impact. ₹20-30 crore; plausibly revenue-neutral-to-positive.

R11 — Municipal property-tax modernisation: the largest untapped urban revenue pool

Builds on: UD&MA at ₹14,609.77 crore and the City Economic Regions scheme [1, p.18]. The gap. KMC property-tax collection is ₹1,258.53 crore against Bengaluru's (BBMP) ₹4,930 crore and Hyderabad's (GHMC) ₹2,012 crore — ~25.5% of Bengaluru's take and ~62.6% of Hyderabad's [15][D15]. Proposal. A GIS-based property census for KMC and the six largest ULBs; unit-area assessment completion; digital billing with early-payment incentives; a published per-ULB collection-efficiency dashboard. Precedent. BBMP's digital assessment-linked collection [15]; GIS property-mapping drives in peer metros lifted assessment coverage double-digit [15]. Viksit Bengal impact. Self-financing cities for the CER programme. Cost/impact. ₹100-150 crore census; the indicative comparator gap is ₹3,000+ crore/yr at Bengaluru-level performance (property-base differences noted) [D15].

R12 — Calcutta Stock Exchange revival: financial-market infrastructure

Builds on: the Budget's explicit commitment to "reclaim Kolkata's place as a financial capital" through CSE revival [1, p.57], alongside SPSU listing plans [1]. The gap. The 118-year-old exchange sits in a regulatory exit/revival process; the pathway (net-worth, platform, governance norms) remains to be executed (press-corroborated status) [77]. Proposal. A time-bound CSE Revival Task Force (Finance Department + regulator liaison + exchange stakeholders): capitalisation plan to meet regulatory norms, a regional SME-listing focus, the state's own SPSU listings as anchor volume. Why. Eastern India lacks a proximate listing venue; an SME-board focus aligns with the 89-lakh-MSME base; the national exchanges' dedicated SME platforms demonstrate the regional-listing demand pool [77]. Viksit Bengal impact. Financial-capital identity restored with measurable KPIs. Cost/impact. Task force ≈₹5 crore; capitalisation via consortium, not budget outlay.
