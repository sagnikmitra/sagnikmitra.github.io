Executive summary

Yuva Shakti is the most ambitious youth-and-sports chapter a WB budget has carried [1]. These eleven proposals supply the phasing, operations funding, pipeline, caps, and evidence-based design that make it land.

Five verified findings: 1. The stake: live jobseeker register 77.6 lakh — India's largest [2]; youth (15-29) unemployment 9.0% (PLFS primary table; M 8.5/F 10.0) vs all-ages 2.5% [3][4]; official graduate out-turn 5,63,911/yr (AISHE Table 33); GER 26.3 vs 28.4; college density 15 vs 30 per lakh [5]. 2. The school base: playgrounds in 68.7% of WB schools vs 83.0% nationally; secondary dropout 20.3% vs 8.2% — ~2.5× [6]; NFHS-5: 41.6% of women 20-24 married before 18; women's anaemia 71% [7]. 3. The runway: sports spend ₹49.50/capita vs Odisha ₹284.88 — 5.75× [8][D1]; SAI block grant ₹56-89 cr/yr and declining; 112 Khelo India Athletes (~3.9%); among the 10 lowest-funded Khelo India states [9]. 4. The footprint awaits activation: KISCE non-operational; 13 civilian KICs all JNV-hosted; 8 Railways KICs non-operational [10]; zero Aspirational Districts [11]. The ₹5 cr/stadium provision is adequate vs the ₹3.90 cr benchmark [12] — operations and siting are the open items. 5. Bengal's own evidence settles the Bhorsha design: the Murshidabad graduation RCT (asset + stipend + coaching) — consumption +~25% at 7 yrs, earnings ~2×, benefit-cost ~379% at 10 yrs (Banerjee et al., Science 2015; J-PAL follow-ups) [13]. Uncapped cost: ₹3,456-5,472 cr/yr at the eligible band; capped at 2 lakh with activation: ≈₹575-650 cr [D2].

I.2 The sports ledger

Demand is documented like nowhere else: Kolkata Derby record crowd 1,31,781 (1997) — India's largest for any sport [19]; Salt Lake 85,000 [20]; Eden Gardens 68,000 [21]; 33 Santosh Trophy titles — the most [22]. Against it: the delivery base measured above [9][10][11]; ₹49.50 vs ₹284.88 per capita [8][D1]; unit economics sound (₹3.90 cr benchmark vs ₹5 cr provision) [12]; and the land constraint, stated neutrally — the historic clubs' grounds sit on defence-administered Maidan land where permanent construction requires case-by-case clearance under the applicable rules, one structural reason academies lag demand [23]; an inter-institutional coordination framework is the unlock (R5).

I.3 The fiscal frame

The state funds at scale when design is settled — Annapurna ₹36,000 cr = exactly 1.00 crore beneficiaries [1][D3]. The youth commitments need the same maturity: announced sports capital ~₹820 cr [D4] vs the ₹490.54 cr year-line resolves by phasing + central shares (R1); Bhorsha — un-allocated in the AFS [1] — needs cap, condition, and costing published before October 2026 (R8); the stress matrix is in Annex 1 [D2].

PART II — ELEVEN PROPOSALS

Format per the group directive. Each opens with the Budget commitment it builds on. Numbered R1-R11.

R1 — Publish the Sports Capital Fund phasing (multi-year, centrally co-financed)

Base: the most ambitious sports capital programme in the state's budget history [1, pp.30-31]. Gap: Named items (~₹820 cr [D4]) exceed the current-year sports provision (₹490.54 cr) — normal for capital programmes; unpublished phasing invites doubt. Do: A published 3-5 year Sports Infrastructure Capital Fund: year-wise build schedule; financing = Khelo India central share + SASCI 50-year interest-free loans (within the Budget's ₹10,000 cr SASCI target [1, p.18]) + state tranche; quarterly completion dashboard. Why: Phasing converts announcements into build schedules; the ₹5 cr/unit provision is already adequate [12] — sequencing is the open variable. Precedent: Odisha's ~₹5,000 crore block-wise sports programme with explicit phasing [24]. Cost: ~₹205 cr/yr over four years [D4], largely offset by central shares.

R2 — Fund stadium O&M through sponsorship ("Adopt-a-Stadium")

Base: the 100-stadium programme and ₹1 cr club assistance [1, p.31]. Gap: No recurring operations provision; unmaintained public sports assets decay in 3-5 years. Do: Long-horizon PSU/corporate naming-rights sponsorship underwriting per-stadium O&M (₹15-25 lakh/yr each) with published accounts and community-programming conditions. Precedent: Odisha Mining Corporation's hockey sponsorship — ₹434.12 crore to 2033 — underwriting recurring costs [25]; the Kalinga high-performance centre's government-corporate operating partnership [26]. Cost: ₹15-25 cr/yr statewide, sponsorship-offset.

R3 — A Sports-Science & Analytics core inside the Sports University

Base: the ₹150 cr Sports University with Sports Science courses named [1, p.30]. Do: Ring-fence ₹30-40 cr for an embedded centre — biomechanics, physiology, a para-athlete lab, recovery suites, a data unit serving every state academy. Why/precedent: Odisha's Kalinga sports-science centre (biomechanics/para/altitude/recovery labs) [27]; JSW's Inspire Institute (300 athletes; 17 Asian Games 2022 medals) [28]; and the system evidence — the UK's target-linked elite funding took Britain from 36th (1996) to 2nd (2016) at a measured ~£4.29m per medal [29]. Cost: Within the ₹150 cr (reallocation).

R4 — Codify the medal-reward and jobs matrix

Base: posts from Inspector to DSP for medal winners and the ₹50 cr prize corpus; the policy is "being drawn up" [1, p.31] — the opportunity. Do: A published medal-tier matrix — fixed cash per tier and defined post per achievement — enacted as policy with annual review against podium outcomes. Precedent: Haryana, the model WB's language mirrors: Olympic Gold ₹6 crore / Silver ₹4 crore / Bronze ₹2.5 crore / participant ₹15 lakh, plus 224 posts with rank mapped to medal [30][31]; Australia's multi-year funding certainty (A$513m across 68 programmes) as the delivery spine [32]. Cost: Anchored to the existing ₹50 cr corpus.

R5 — Block-level talent identification + an inter-institutional Maidan framework

Base: the mass-participation intent of the Khelo India rollout [1, p.30] and club assistance [1, p.31]. Do: (a) District-to-block talent search: hostels for scouted children (10-14), a digitised talent portal (8-17), one nursery per block through BDOs. (b) A coordination framework among the state, the land-administering defence authorities, and the clubs, enabling long-lease academy infrastructure on or near club grounds under the applicable land rules — a convening act, not a budget line [23]. Why: Grassroots scouting fills the stadiums being built; the school data (playgrounds 68.7%; dropout 20.3% [6]) makes school-linked scouting a retention tool too. Bengal's 33 Santosh titles [22] and the Derby's record crowd [19] are demand proof. Precedent: Odisha's Sports Hostel Scheme (since 1985) and block-level talent search [33]. Cost: ₹50-75 cr phased; the framework near-zero.

R6 — Scale the startup funds toward peer size; an honest two-track Banglar Udyam

Base: the startup policy (₹40 cr incubation + ₹60 cr venture funds), the ₹50 cr deep-tech talent fund [1, p.56], Banglar Udyam (₹200 cr) [1, p.55], and 1,000-school Atal Tinkering Labs (₹200 cr) [1, p.26]. Gap: Peer scale: Gujarat's student-startup policy runs a ₹500 cr corpus (1,543 startups through 339 institutions) [34]; Kerala's ecosystem spans 550 campus incubators and ₹6,000 cr of facilitated funding [35]. WB's certified count is 4,627 [17]; Kolkata's reported cumulative venture funding over the past decade (~US$250 million) is of the order of what Bengaluru attracts in a single month (US$3.3 billion across 2024) [36]. Do: (a) A five-year path from ₹100 cr to a ₹500 cr Bengal Startup Fund-of-Funds co-investing with SEBI-registered funds. (b) Banglar Udyam split into an Idea-to-PoC grant track (₹25-50 lakh) plus a guarantee-leveraged loan track (Set F, R2). (c) Delivery through campus incubators riding the ATL school feeder [1, p.26]. Precedent: Gujarat SSIP 2.0 [34]; Kerala Startup Mission [35]; Karnataka ELEVATE (₹224 cr → 983 startups) [37]. Cost: Year-1 cost-neutral; +₹80-100 cr/yr on the five-year path.

R7 — One skill-certification and job-matching platform across ₹3,206 crore

Base: the largest skilling stack in the state's history: Technical Education ₹2,393.20 cr, PM-SETU ITI modernisation ₹570 cr, AVGC-XR ₹50 cr, free district coaching, the GCC policy, Self-Employment ₹322.80 cr [1][D5]. Gap: Separate rails; no unified certification, placement tracking, or employer co-governance — while the migration driver is job quality [18]. Do: One state platform: nationally recognised certification, employer-co-designed curricula, published placement rates, the dual-model principle (trainees inside firms on paid contracts), the GCC policy as anchor demand. Why: Germany's dual system (~430,000 participating firms) retains ~68% of trainees at their training firm, with a youth-unemployment rate far below the EU average of 14.9% [38]; Singapore's SkillsFuture demonstrates portable credits with measured re-employment (55% of previously-unemployed trainees within six months) [39]. Precedent: Kerala ASAP (2.5 lakh+ certified) [40]; Telangana TASK (industry cohorts since 2014) [41]. Cost: ₹50-75 cr, pooled across departments.

R8 — Bhorsha Karmasuchi as "Cash + Activation": capped, costed, published — with a graduation-approach pilot

Base: the named, dated commitment — from October 2026; ₹3,000/month graduates, ₹2,000 others; ages 21-45; family income below ₹1 lakh [1, p.32] — the state's first structured youth-support scheme since Yuvasree. Gap: No allocation is attached in the Annual Financial Statement [1]; demand is verifiably large: register 77.6 lakh [2]; filtered eligible band ~12-19 lakh [D2]; uncapped cost ₹3,456-5,472 crore/yr [D2]. Do: Before launch: (a) a published cap with eligibility counts from the register; (b) a skilling/work condition routed through the R7 platform; (c) a four-month activation guarantee — an offer of work, training, or apprenticeship within four months of enrolment; (d) a graduation-approach pilot (50,000 beneficiaries): productive asset + stipend + coaching, per the published research design; (e) an outcome-linked review clause. Why: Across 200+ active-labour-market programme evaluations, training-centred designs outperform pure transfers at 2-3 year horizons (Card, Kluve & Weber 2018) [42]; the EU Youth Guarantee's four-month activation offer has recorded 63 million take-ups since 2013 [43]; and the randomised evaluation conducted in Murshidabad measured consumption +25% at seven years, roughly doubled earnings, and ~379% benefit-cost at ten years for the graduation design (Banerjee et al. 2015; long-run follow-ups) [13]. Comparable state schemes cap: Rajasthan's allowance (capped ~2 lakh, internship-conditioned, ~₹866 crore/yr) [44]; WB's own Yuvasree (capped at 1 lakh) set the precedent at home [45]. Cost: Capped at 2 lakh + condition: ≈₹575-650 crore/yr, bounded and outcome-linked [D2]; the graduation pilot ≈₹250-300 crore one-time (cost-scaled from the published design; indicative) [13].

R9 — AVGC-XR anchored in a Centre of Excellence + a permanent content agency

Base: ₹50 cr AVGC-XR (1 lakh trainees/yr, 500 Content Creator Labs, WEBEL revamp) [1, p.30]; the GCC policy [1, p.57]. Gap: Training without an industry anchor; Kolkata's current base is narrower than assumed — a major studio operates locally [46], Tollygunge produced 145 films (2023) [47], but no published animation-workforce count exists; the collapsed ~3,200-artist Technicolor India operation was Bengaluru/Mumbai-based — displaced talent Bengal can attract [48]. Do: (a) A physical Centre of Excellence (studios + incubation + industry residency) with a local-hire incentive; (b) a permanent content agency (funding, export promotion, an annual data white paper); (c) a displaced-talent attraction window on Kolkata's cost advantage. Why: India's AVGC-XR market is projected from ~US$3bn to US$26bn+ by 2030, with direct jobs from 2.6 lakh to ~23 lakh by 2032 [49]; the state-agency model anchors Korea's ₩23.85 trillion, ~87,000-job games industry [50]. Precedent: Telangana's IMAGE policy (first state AVGC policy; ~30,000 VFX jobs) [51]; Karnataka AVGC-XR 3.0 [52]. Cost: Within the ₹50 cr + studio co-investment; agency ₹10-15 cr/yr.

R10 — A women-in-sports pipeline and a sports-tourism strategy on the Budget's own assets

Base: the strongest women's programme in state history (Annapurna ₹36,000 cr; ₹1,000 cr girl-UG grants; ₹550 cr free bus travel) [1]; stadium assets; the Durga Puja global-branding campaign [1, p.61]; WB's 18.5 crore annual tourist footfall (31 lakh foreign — 2nd nationally) [53]. Gap: No women-specific sports provision — while female LFPR is 33.8% and 66.7% of working women are self-employed [4]; and no sports-event economics strategy. Do: (a) A women's-sports pipeline — scholarships, hostels, coaching, reserved slots in R5 — with an equity condition (published girls'-participation ratios) on every state sports grant; (b) one signature annual event (a Kolkata heritage riverfront marathon; a Darjeeling endurance circuit) engineered for multi-night visitors, cross-marketed with the Durga Puja campaign; planned on measured incremental spend, not footfall multipliers (the discipline the Kashi/Ayodhya numbers teach: large visit counts are footfall-derived estimates, not audited revenue) [54]. Why: Women's sport is the highest-ROI medal lane — women delivered ~72% of Australia's Paris 2024 golds after record women's-sport investment [55]; Title IX's condition-based access grew US girls' participation ~990% [56]. Sports tourism is a ~US$645bn market growing ~11%/yr [57]; Singapore's Grand Prix banked S$2.2bn of incremental receipts from 720,000 visitors by designing for high-spend stays [58]. Precedent: Khelo India ASMITA women's leagues (~3 lakh participants) [59]; Sport England's This Girl Can (2.8m women activated) [60]. Cost: Pipeline ₹30-50 cr; the event strategy leverages existing assets.

R11 — Activate the Khelo India footprint with formula-based siting

Base: the 100-constituency stadium programme [1, p.30]. Gap: The KISCE non-operational; 13 civilian KICs JNV-hosted; 8 Railways KICs non-operational [10]; WB among the ten lowest-funded Khelo India states, SAI block grant declining [9]; no Aspirational-District channel [11]. Do: (a) A joint state-SAI activation plan: revive the KISCE, accredit a first tranche of new stadiums as KICs (unlocking central operating support), open JNV-hosted KICs to community hours. (b) Site the 100 stadiums by a published formula — population + district-income-inverse + facility-gap — placing the first tranche by the formula-derived cohort combining low district income and measured facility gaps (Birbhum, Uttar Dinajpur, Purulia, Malda, Dakshin Dinajpur), with Jhargram surveyed first as a data-gap district [61]. Why: Accreditation converts state capex into centrally co-supported operations; formula siting is the audit-proof answer to "where do the 100 go?" Precedent: The KIC operating-support model itself [10]; Odisha's block-formula rollout [24]. Cost: Near-zero incremental; unlocks central O&M support.

PART V — METHODOLOGY & SOURCE INTEGRITY

Identical discipline to the companion Finance dossier: the Budget is the primary source [1]; 32 structured investigations supply the evidence (primary dashboards, DGE register statistics, the PLFS primary state table, AISHE/UDISE+/NFHS official tables, the randomised-evaluation literature, comparative budget analyses, court records, vernacular press, labelled sentiment). Every load-bearing figure is citation-keyed and tiered; the graduate out-turn now uses the official AISHE table (5,63,911), which supersedes an earlier derived estimate (~6.7 lakh) — a correction recorded in the project log [5]. Conflicts are presented, not averaged. Items needing physical/RTI access are in Annex 2. Presentation conventions follow the adapted government-document style guide; no official emblem or marker of government origin is used [63].
